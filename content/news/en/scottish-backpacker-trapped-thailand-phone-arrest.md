A 37-year-old Scottish backpacker from Edinburgh, **Rory McColl**, is stuck in Thailand unable to leave the country after his arrest in Bangkok for allegedly stealing a mobile phone, which he insists was an innocent mistake. The incident occurred just hours after his arrival in early March on Khao San Road, a bustling nightlife area, and has left him in legal limbo with a passport seizure and a court date set for late July, forcing him to remain in the country for at least three more months.

## The Incident Unfolds

Rory McColl arrived in Bangkok after a 30-hour journey from Scotland and headed straight to Khao San Road, the iconic backpacker hub known for its vibrant bars and street life. According to details shared with [BBC](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c1e2pvygv20o), shortly after settling in for drinks at a bar, McColl realized he had misplaced his own phone. Spotting what he believed was his device on the bar counter, he picked it up and pocketed it without hesitation—only to discover later that it belonged to a waitress.

Bar staff called the police, leading to McColl's arrest between 9 and 10 March at Chanasongkhram police station. He spent two nights on the concrete floor of a police cell before being formally charged with theft and having his passport confiscated. McColl immediately explained to officers that it was a simple case of mistaken identity due to the identical phones, and he has since communicated directly with the phone's owner. The BBC reviewed an email exchange where the woman appeared to support his account, acknowledging the misunderstanding, though she has not responded to further messages from him. However, a police officer at the station confirmed the arrest but stated there was no record of any misunderstanding in their files, emphasizing that "the offence has been done."

This case underscores the swift enforcement of Thai law on petty theft, even in scenarios that seem resolvable through dialogue. Under Thai regulations, such a charge can carry a potential jail sentence of up to five years, though outcomes often depend on the court's assessment of intent and evidence.

## Family's Ordeal and Legal Proceedings

McColl's family back in Scotland has been deeply affected by the situation. His mother, **Helen McColl**, described the ordeal to BBC Scotland News as "inhumane," noting that her son feels like he is already serving a prison sentence, isolated thousands of miles from home. His parents, Helen and John, speak with him daily but express frustration over his indefinite stay. Currently free on bail, McColl has been living in a hotel in Pattaya, approximately 90 miles (150 km) southeast of Bangkok, for the past seven weeks. Pattaya, a popular coastal resort town for travelers seeking a break from the capital's intensity, has become an unintended base as he awaits resolution.

A court hearing took place in Bangkok on Tuesday, where McColl's lawyer presented mitigation, including evidence from the waitress confirming the mix-up. Despite this, the judge deferred the decision on his not guilty plea to a future date: 28 July. At that time, the court will review the plea and determine if any punishment is warranted. The British Foreign Office is providing support to McColl as a UK resident arrested abroad and is in regular contact with Thai authorities to monitor the case.

The family's account highlights the emotional toll of such bureaucratic delays. Helen McColl told reporters that the separation and uncertainty have been particularly hard, with Rory unable to return home or even travel freely within Thailand due to the bail conditions.

This incident serves as a cautionary tale for backpackers and tourists navigating Thailand's dynamic travel scene. Pattaya, another hotspot for extended stays, offers beaches and nightlife but may feel isolating for those stuck due to legal issues—travelers there should be aware of local support networks for expats and visitors in distress.

For those planning trips, keeping track of belongings in high-traffic areas is crucial. Thailand's legal system treats theft seriously, even if unintentional, and misunderstandings can escalate quickly without immediate resolution. ## What's Next for Rory McColl

Looking ahead, McColl's fate hinges on the 28 July hearing, where the court will evaluate his not guilty plea and the submitted evidence. If the judge accepts the misunderstanding narrative, he could avoid punishment and regain his passport, allowing a return to Scotland. However, a conviction, even minor, might result in fines or a short jail term, extending his time in Thailand further.

His family remains hopeful but vigilant, urging UK authorities to advocate on his behalf. For now, McColl continues his wait in Pattaya, a reminder of how a small mishap can disrupt travel plans in unexpected ways. Travelers to Thailand are encouraged to stay cautious in nightlife districts like Khao San Road and carry copies of important documents to mitigate similar risks.

This case, while isolated, prompts reflection on traveler safety in one of Southeast Asia's top destinations. As Thailand welcomes millions annually, stories like McColl's emphasize the importance of clear communication and awareness of local laws to ensure smooth journeys.
