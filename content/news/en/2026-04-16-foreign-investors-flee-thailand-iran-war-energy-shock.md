# Foreign Investors Flee Thailand as Iran War Sparks Energy Shock

Foreign investors are selling off Thai assets, according to a **Reuters** report from Singapore on April 16, 2026, as an energy shock triggered by the U.S.-Israeli war on Iran dashes hopes for Thailand's economic revival.

## Background on Thailand's Economic Vulnerabilities

Thailand's economy has long depended on foreign direct investment (FDI) to fuel growth, particularly in manufacturing, tourism infrastructure, and export-oriented industries. Publicly known data from sources like the Bank of Thailand and the Board of Investment show that FDI inflows have been crucial for job creation and technology transfer. In recent years leading up to 2026, the country had been eyeing a post-pandemic recovery, with tourism rebounding and manufacturing hubs in the Eastern Economic Corridor attracting electronics and automotive firms.

However, the reference point from Reuters highlights a sudden shift: investors are fleeing Thai assets amid geopolitical turmoil. The U.S.-Israeli war on Iran, a widely reported conflict by 2026, has disrupted global energy markets. Iran, a major oil producer, faces sanctions and supply interruptions, leading to what the report calls an "energy shock." This manifests as spiking oil prices, which Thailand imports almost entirely, making the kingdom sensitive to such events. Historically, Thailand's economy has suffered during oil crises, like in the 1970s and 2008, when higher fuel costs eroded trade balances and consumer spending.

No specific figures on the scale of outflows are provided in the available data, so the extent remains unclear—whether it's bonds, stocks on the Stock Exchange of Thailand (SET), or direct investments. What is known is the timing: April 2026, when revival hopes were high after years of sluggish growth around 2-3% annually, per widely known IMF projections.

## Broader Economic Implications

The energy shock compounds Thailand's challenges. Higher energy costs ripple through transportation, manufacturing, and agriculture—key pillars. Factories in industrial estates like those in Chonburi or Rayong, known hubs for foreign firms, face elevated operational expenses, potentially leading to production cuts or relocations. The baht, already volatile, could weaken further against the dollar as investors seek safer havens, increasing import bills.

For tourism, which accounts for around 12% of GDP in pre-2026 public data, the indirect effects are notable. Airlines and hotels, major energy users, might pass on costs, though no direct investor flight from tourism assets is specified. The Reuters report frames this as a broader dash to economic revival hopes, suggesting investor confidence is waning at a critical juncture. Thailand's government has promoted incentives like tax breaks for EV and digital sectors, but global shocks override local policies.

It's worth noting the source's focus: "foreign investors are selling Thai assets." This likely refers to portfolio flows out of the SET index or bonds, rather than wholesale FDI pullouts, as the latter takes longer. Still, sentiment drives markets, and panic selling can self-fulfill downturns.

## What's Next for Thailand's Economy

Uncertainty looms. Diplomatic efforts to de-escalate the Iran conflict could ease the energy shock, stabilizing markets. Thailand might accelerate renewables—solar and offshore wind projects are publicly known initiatives—to reduce oil dependence. Central bank interventions to support the baht or stimulus packages could stem outflows.

Investors may pause rather than fully exit, given Thailand's strategic location in ASEAN and trade pacts like CPTPP.
