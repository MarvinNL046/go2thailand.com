One of Bangkok's most iconic temples is fighting back against aggressive commercial photographers who have been making visitors feel unwelcome. **[Wat Arun](https://go2-thailand.com/blog/wat-arun-bangkok-temple-of-dawn-guide/)** (Temple of the Dawn) has launched a multi-agency crackdown on disruptive photography behavior, requiring all on-site photographers to complete mandatory conduct training.

## What Was Happening

The problem centered on **commercial costume photographers** — operators linked to nearby rental shops who offer tourists traditional Thai costume photo sessions at the temple. According to [ThailandBlog.nl](https://www.thailandblog.nl/nieuws-uit-thailand/wat-arun-in-bangkok-pakt-onbeschofte-fotografen-aan-om-tempelbezoek-weer-rustig-te-maken/), complaints from both Thai and international visitors had been increasing about photographers who:

- **Asked visitors to move** out of their shooting areas, effectively claiming public temple space as private sets
- **Pressured tourists to leave** certain photogenic spots so their paying clients could use them
- **Made non-customers feel unwelcome** — visitors who didn't hire a photographer reported feeling pushed aside

The behavior was undermining the temple's spiritual atmosphere and turning a sacred site into what felt like a commercial photo studio.

## The New Rules

Wat Arun's response involves multiple agencies working together — a sign that authorities are taking the issue seriously:

- **Temple administration** has established new conduct rules for all on-site photographers
- **Mandatory behavior training** covers correct conduct, etiquette, and compliance with temple regulations
- **Local police and tourist police** are involved in enforcement
- **Bangkok Yai district officials** oversee compliance

The key enforcement mechanism: **photographers who don't follow the rules lose their permission to work on temple grounds**. No permit means no business — a powerful deterrent for commercial operators.

## Why This Matters for Temple Visitors

If you've visited popular Thai temples recently, you may have noticed the costume photography trend. Visitors (particularly from China, Korea, and increasingly Western countries) rent elaborate Thai costumes and hire photographers for professional photo sessions among the temple's stunning architecture.

The practice itself isn't the problem — it can be a beautiful way to experience Thai culture. The issue was photographers who prioritized their commercial interests over other visitors' experiences.

Under the new rules, you should notice:

- **Less crowding pressure** at popular photo spots within the temple
- **No one asking you to move** so a paying client can shoot
- **A calmer, more respectful atmosphere** overall
- **Commercial photography still available** — just conducted respectfully

## Broader Temple Etiquette Reminder

Wat Arun's action is part of a wider trend of Thai temples tightening visitor conduct rules. For travelers visiting any Thai temple in 2026:

- **Dress code**: Cover shoulders and knees. Many temples provide sarongs at the entrance, but bringing your own avoids delays
- **Shoes off**: Remove footwear before entering any temple building
- **Quiet voice**: Temples are active places of worship, not just tourist attractions
- **Don't point feet at Buddha images** — sit with legs tucked under or to the side
- **Ask before photographing monks** — some welcome it, others don't
- **Women should not touch monks** or hand them objects directly

## Visiting Wat Arun

Wat Arun remains one of Bangkok's must-visit sites. Entry costs **100 THB** (free for Thai nationals). The temple is open daily **8:00 AM - 5:30 PM**.

The best way to reach it: take the **cross-river ferry from Tha Tien pier** (near Wat Pho) for just **4 THB** — one of Bangkok's best-value experiences.
