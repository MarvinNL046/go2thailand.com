A 37-year-old Scottish backpacker from Edinburgh, **Rory McColl**, is stuck in Thailand facing up to three months in legal limbo after being arrested for allegedly stealing a mobile phone that he insists was an innocent mistake. The incident occurred just hours after his arrival in [Bangkok](https://go2-thailand.com/city/bangkok/) in early March 2026, at a bar on the famous Khao San Road nightlife strip, leading to his passport being seized and a court hearing postponed until 28 July.

## The Incident Unfolds

Rory McColl had just completed a grueling 30-hour journey from Scotland when he settled into the vibrant atmosphere of Khao San Road, a bustling backpacker hub known for its bars and street life. According to details shared by his family and confirmed by police, McColl realized he had misplaced his phone during drinks at the bar. Spotting what he believed to be his own device on the counter—an identical model—he picked it up and pocketed it without hesitation.

Bar staff soon alerted authorities, and McColl was arrested between 9 and 10 March at Chanasongkhram police station in Bangkok. He spent two nights on the concrete floor of a police cell before being formally charged with theft. His passport was confiscated, preventing him from leaving the country. McColl maintains that it was a simple misunderstanding, explaining the situation to officers upon his arrest. He has also communicated directly with the phone's owner, a waitress at the bar, who initially appeared to accept his account in email exchanges viewed by the BBC. However, she has since stopped responding.

A police officer at the station confirmed the arrest and the theft accusation to BBC News but dismissed claims of a misunderstanding, stating there was no record of it in their files and that "the offence has been done." Under Thai law, such a theft charge carries a potential penalty of up to five years in jail, though outcomes can vary based on circumstances.

## Family's Ordeal and Legal Proceedings

McColl's parents, Helen and John McColl, have been vocal about their son's predicament from their home in Scotland. Speaking to BBC Scotland News, Helen described the situation as "inhumane," noting that Rory is effectively serving a prison sentence far from family support. The family speaks with him daily, but they feel he is trapped in limbo, unable to plan his return or move freely.

Currently free on bail, McColl has been residing in a hotel in [Pattaya](https://go2-thailand.com/city/pattaya/), about 90 miles (150 km) southeast of Bangkok, for the past seven weeks. This coastal city, popular among tourists for its beaches and nightlife, has become an unintended base as he awaits resolution. At a court hearing in Bangkok on Tuesday—prior to the article's publication on 28 April—his lawyer presented mitigation evidence, including the waitress's apparent acknowledgment that it was a mix-up. The court accepted his not guilty plea but deferred a final judgment to the 28 July hearing, where it will decide on any punishment.

The UK's Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office (FCDO) is providing support to McColl as a UK resident arrested abroad and is in contact with local Thai authorities. This assistance includes guidance on legal processes and welfare checks, though specifics remain confidential.

## Implications for Travelers in Thailand

This case underscores the potential pitfalls for backpackers and tourists in Thailand, particularly in high-energy spots like Khao San Road. Miscommunications over personal items, especially in crowded bars where alcohol is involved, can escalate quickly due to language barriers and strict enforcement of laws. Thailand's legal system treats theft seriously, even for minor incidents, and foreign visitors risk passport seizures that halt travel plans indefinitely.

For those exploring Bangkok's vibrant scene or heading to nearby areas like Pattaya, vigilance with belongings is key. The incident highlights the importance of double-checking items in social settings and promptly resolving any mix-ups with staff or owners. While McColl's story appears to stem from an honest error, the lack of recorded acknowledgment from the complainant or police has prolonged his ordeal. Travelers should familiarize themselves with [Thailand's practical travel info](https://go2-thailand.com/practical-info/), including what to do if detained, to navigate such situations.

Broader context from similar reports suggests that while Thailand remains a welcoming destination for millions of visitors annually, isolated cases like this can amplify concerns about legal entanglements. No widespread pattern of phone-related arrests is indicated, but it serves as a cautionary tale amid the country's post-pandemic tourism recovery.

## What's Next for Rory McColl

The upcoming 28 July court date looms large for McColl, who could face anything from a fine to a jail term if his not guilty plea is rejected. His family hopes the mitigation evidence, including the email corroboration—though its authenticity couldn't be independently verified by the BBC—will sway the judge toward leniency. Until then, he remains barred from departing Thailand, extending his stay well beyond his original plans.

For visitors, this story reinforces the need for caution in nightlife areas and awareness of local laws.
