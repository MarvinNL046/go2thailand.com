A 37-year-old father from Edinburgh, **Rory McColl**, is stranded in Thailand and could face up to three years in jail after what he describes as a simple mistake: picking up the wrong mobile phone in a Bangkok bar on the first night of his backpacking holiday. Arriving in the country on 9 March for a planned 12-day trip, McColl was arrested shortly after, spent two nights in a concrete cell, and has since been living in a Pattaya hotel under bail conditions with his passport confiscated. The incident unfolded on the lively Khao San Road, a famous backpacker hub in [Bangkok](https://go2-thailand.com/city/bangkok/), and highlights potential pitfalls for travelers in Thailand's nightlife scenes.

## The Incident Unfolds

Rory McColl, originally from Scotland, had just completed a grueling 30-hour journey when he decided to unwind with drinks at a bar on Khao San Road. According to details shared with his family and reported by [BBC](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c87wz7n5pwqo), McColl realized he had misplaced his own phone during the evening. Spotting what appeared to be an identical device on the bar, he picked it up and pocketed it without realizing it belonged to another person—a woman nearby.

Bar staff noticed the mix-up and alerted the police, leading to McColl's swift arrest. He was handcuffed and taken to a local station, where he spent two nights in what his parents described as horrendous conditions: a concrete floor with only a plastic water bottle as a pillow. McColl explained the situation to officers, emphasizing it was an honest error, and even communicated with the phone's owner. The BBC reviewed an email exchange in which the woman seemed to support his account, though its authenticity could not be independently verified. However, she has since ceased responding to his messages, and Thai police are proceeding with theft charges regardless.

This case underscores how quickly misunderstandings can escalate in Thailand, particularly in bustling tourist areas like Khao San Road, known for its vibrant street life and international visitors. McColl, a father back home in Edinburgh, was simply trying to enjoy the start of his adventure but found himself entangled in the local legal system.

## Family's Ordeal and Suspect Legal Help

McColl's parents, **Helen** and **John McColl**, have been in daily contact with their son via FaceTime, offering emotional support from Scotland. Helen recounted to BBC Scotland how the events unfolded rapidly: "It all happened so quickly—before Rory knew it, the police were there." They expressed deep concern over his well-being, especially after hearing about the jail conditions and his growing distress when he realized he wouldn't be released immediately.

After two nights in custody, McColl was granted bail for around £1,000, but his passport was seized, preventing him from leaving Thailand. He relocated to a hotel in [Pattaya](https://go2-thailand.com/city/pattaya/), a coastal resort town about 90 miles southeast of Bangkok, where he has remained for the past seven weeks. Pattaya, popular among beachgoers and backpackers, has become an unintended base for McColl as he awaits resolution.

Complicating matters further, while in custody, McColl was approached by a man claiming to be a lawyer offering court representation. McColl paid the individual twice, hoping for assistance to return home to his son. However, his family grew suspicious when the man advised McColl to travel two hours to court only to send him away, citing a "bad judge" that day. No progress was made, and the family's alarm bells rang louder with each unfulfilled promise. This raises questions about the reliability of unsolicited legal aid in such situations, a common caution for foreigners navigating Thailand's justice system. The McColls' worry is palpable; Helen noted their son was "distraught" upon realizing the severity of his predicament. As parents, they are doing everything possible from afar, but the distance amplifies their anxiety ahead of the upcoming proceedings.

## Implications for Travelers in Thailand

This incident serves as a stark reminder of the importance of vigilance in Thailand's tourist hotspots. Khao San Road, with its bars and crowds, attracts millions of backpackers annually, but petty mix-ups can lead to serious consequences under Thai law. Theft charges, even if stemming from a misunderstanding, carry penalties of up to three years in prison, as outlined in the country's strict criminal code. Travelers often underestimate how seriously such matters are treated, especially when alcohol is involved or when bar staff intervene.

For visitors, the story highlights the need for caution with personal belongings. Keeping phones secure and double-checking items in busy environments can prevent similar errors. Additionally, it points to broader challenges for foreigners facing legal troubles in Thailand, including language barriers, unfamiliar procedures, and potential scams from opportunistic "helpers." Official channels, such as contacting one's embassy, are recommended over unverified lawyers. ## What's Next for Rory McColl

A preliminary court hearing is scheduled for 28 April, where the charges could be dropped if the judge finds merit in McColl's explanation. Until then, he remains in limbo in Pattaya, unable to return to Scotland or reunite with his family. Police continue to pursue the case, despite the apparent corroboration from the phone's owner, leaving the outcome uncertain.

For McColl and his parents, the wait is agonizing. As Helen put it, they speak every day, clinging to hope that the "mistake" will be recognized as such. This saga not only affects one family but serves as a cautionary tale for the global community of backpackers drawn to Thailand's allure. Travelers are urged to stay alert, document interactions, and seek verified help if issues arise, ensuring their adventures remain just that—adventures, not ordeals.
