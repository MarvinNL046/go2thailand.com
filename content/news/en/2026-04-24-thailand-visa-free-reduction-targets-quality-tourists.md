# Thailand Weighs Cutting Visa-Free Access from 93 Countries to 57

Thailand's tourism authorities are evaluating a significant policy shift that could reduce the number of countries eligible for visa-free entry from **93** to **57**, with the goal of attracting 'higher-quality visitors' and promoting long-stay tourism. This potential change, which would scrap visa-free access for **36** countries, was highlighted in a recent report published by [Thaiger](https://thethaiger.com/guides/visa-information/thailand-considers-visa-free-policy-changes-to-target-quality-and-long-stay-tourists) on April 23, 2026.

## Background on Thailand's Visa-Free Policy

Thailand has long relied on visa exemptions to boost its tourism industry, one of the country's economic pillars. The current policy allows nationals from **93** countries to enter without a prior visa for short stays, facilitating easy access for millions of visitors annually. This expansive list reflects Thailand's post-pandemic strategy to revive tourism numbers after years of restrictions.

According to the Thaiger report, officials are now contemplating a reduction to just **57** countries. The difference—**36** nations—highlights a targeted approach to refine visitor demographics. The emphasis on 'higher-quality visitors' suggests a preference for tourists who contribute more economically, stay longer, and perhaps engage in higher-spending activities like cultural tours, wellness retreats, or extended holidays in destinations such as [Bangkok](https://go2-thailand.com/city/bangkok/) or [Phuket](https://go2-thailand.com/city/phuket/).

No specific countries have been named in the report for removal from the list, leaving details unclear at this stage. The policy weighs in the context of Thailand's broader tourism recovery, where volume has rebounded but authorities seek sustainable growth. Long-stay tourists, often retirees or digital nomads, align with initiatives like extended visa options already available, such as the Long-Term Resident (LTR) visa.

## Broader Implications for Thailand's Tourism Sector

Thailand's tourism landscape, as reflected in recent Thaiger coverage, grapples with diverse challenges. Stories of abandoned rental motorcycles at Phuket airport and parasailing accidents underscore operational issues, while positive notes like Chiang Mai's biohacking acclaim highlight strengths. A visa policy tweak fits into efforts to elevate the sector.

Reducing visa-free access could streamline border processes and reduce administrative burdens, focusing resources on high-value arrivals. Long-stay tourists often spend on accommodations, dining, and experiences, supporting local economies in northern Thailand or the south. However, it risks alienating markets that drive volume, especially if Asian or emerging nations are cut.

The report does not specify timelines or responsible bodies—likely the Tourism Authority of Thailand (TAT) or Immigration Bureau—but aligns with government priorities for sustainable tourism.
