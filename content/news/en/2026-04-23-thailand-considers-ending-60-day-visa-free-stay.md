Thailand's **Ministry of Tourism and Sports** is preparing to propose a review of the **60-day visa-free entry scheme** to the Cabinet, aiming to attract higher-quality tourists who spend more during their stays. According to [Khaosod English](https://www.khaosodenglish.com/featured/2026/04/22/thailand-considers-ending-60-day-visa-free-stay-eyes-higher-quality-tourists/), this move reflects a strategic pivot in tourism policy, focusing on quality over quantity amid ongoing efforts to balance economic benefits with sustainable visitor management. The proposal comes as Thailand continues to recover from pandemic-era tourism slumps, with no specific timeline announced yet for the Cabinet's decision.

## Background on the 60-Day Visa-Free Scheme

The **60-day visa-free entry scheme** has been a cornerstone of Thailand's tourism revival strategy. Introduced to boost visitor numbers post-COVID-19, it allows nationals from numerous countries to enter Thailand without a visa for up to 60 days, extendable in some cases. This policy, widely promoted through [Thailand's visa information page](https://go2-thailand.com/visa/), has drawn millions annually to hotspots like [Bangkok](https://go2-thailand.com/city/bangkok/), Phuket, and Chiang Mai, contributing significantly to the economy—tourism accounts for a substantial portion of GDP through hotels, food, and transport.

However, the scheme has faced criticism for attracting longer-stay visitors who may spend less per day compared to short-term leisure travelers. Reports of digital nomads, budget backpackers, and others lingering for extended periods have sparked debates on whether it dilutes revenue from high-spending tourists. The **Ministry of Tourism and Sports** now seeks a formal review, signaling potential adjustments to entry rules. While details remain sparse, the intent is clear: shift toward visitors who prioritize premium experiences, such as luxury resorts on islands like [Phuket](https://go2-thailand.com/islands/phuket/) or cultural tours in the north.

This isn't the first policy tweak. Thailand has experimented with visa exemptions, extensions, and digital nomad visas in recent years to test demand. The current proposal builds on that, responding to industry calls for sustainable tourism models. Overcrowding at popular sites, environmental strain, and uneven economic distribution—where beach areas thrive but rural spots lag—have fueled the push for change. ## What It Means for Travelers

For travelers, a review or potential end to the **60-day visa-free stay** could mean stricter entry requirements. Visa-free visitors might face shortened stays, perhaps reverting to 30 days, or mandatory visa applications via embassies or e-visa systems. Budget travelers and long-term stays, popular among Europeans, Americans, and Australians eligible under the scheme, would be most affected. Families planning extended holidays or remote workers basing in Thailand could need alternatives like tourist visas, which require more paperwork and fees.

High-end tourists, however, might see little disruption—or even benefits—if new policies introduce tailored visas for luxury segments. Airlines, hotels, and tour operators catering to premium markets could gain, but low-cost carriers and hostels might suffer. International visitors should monitor updates closely, as changes could impact spontaneous trips to Thailand's vibrant cities and beaches. For now, the scheme remains in place, but planning ahead via [go2thailand.com/visa/](https://go2-thailand.com/visa/) is advisable.

The broader implication ties into Thailand's tourism diversification. With competition from Vietnam, Indonesia, and Japan, emphasizing quality could elevate Thailand's global image, much like Singapore's focus on high-value visitors. Yet, it risks alienating mass-market tourists who fuel volume-based revenue.

## Government's Rationale and Challenges

The drive for "higher-quality tourists" stems from economic imperatives. Thailand's tourism sector relies on spending power; data shows luxury visitors contribute disproportionately to luxury retail, spas, and fine dining. Long-stay, low-spend groups, while numerous, strain infrastructure without proportional returns. The ministry's proposal aligns with national goals for sustainable growth, addressing overtourism issues seen in places like Maya Bay or Phi Phi Islands.

Challenges abound, though. Implementing changes requires international coordination, as visa policies affect dozens of nationalities. Backlash from tourism-dependent businesses, especially in [Phuket](https://go2-thailand.com/islands/phuket/) and [Krabi](https://go2-thailand.com/city/krabi/), is likely. Economically, Thailand must avoid deterring the 30-40 million annual visitors that sustain jobs.

The **Ministry of Tourism and Sports** will submit the proposal to the Cabinet soon, though no exact date is confirmed. If approved, public consultations and regulatory tweaks could follow, with changes possibly effective in months. Travelers should watch official announcements from the Tourism Authority of Thailand.
