# Thailand Cancels 2001 Maritime Deal with Cambodia After Deadlock

BANGKOK — Thailand has formally abandoned a 2001 memorandum of understanding on maritime boundary delimitation with Cambodia, citing prolonged deadlock in negotiations over territorial claims in the Gulf of Thailand. The decision, announced by Thai officials, marks the end of over two decades of diplomatic efforts to resolve overlapping exclusive economic zones (EEZs) rich in potential oil and gas resources. According to **AP News**, this step comes amid ongoing tensions between the neighboring countries, which have historically strained relations due to border disputes.

The scrapped deal, originally signed in 2001, aimed to establish a framework for joint development of the disputed maritime areas. However, progress stalled due to disagreements on boundary lines and resource-sharing mechanisms. Thailand's move to terminate the agreement reflects frustration with the lack of advancement, potentially opening the door for unilateral actions or renewed international mediation.

## Background on the Maritime Dispute

The roots of the Thailand-Cambodia maritime disagreement trace back to colonial-era boundaries that left ambiguities in the Gulf of Thailand. Both nations claim portions of the seabed extending up to 200 nautical miles from their coastlines, as per the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS). The 2001 memorandum was intended as a cooperative starting point, but negotiations faltered repeatedly.

Key sticking points included the delineation around islands like **Koh Kut** in Thailand and **Koh Kong** in Cambodia, where claims overlap significantly. These areas are believed to hold substantial hydrocarbon reserves, making the dispute economically charged. Past efforts, including bilateral talks and involvement from international bodies, have yielded little beyond temporary ceasefires in exploration activities.

Thailand's decision to scrap the deal follows a pattern of escalating rhetoric. In recent years, both governments have accused each other of violating agreements, with Cambodia pushing for arbitration through the International Court of Justice (ICJ), a body already familiar with their land border conflicts, such as the 2013 ruling on the Preah Vihear temple area. While the maritime issue remains separate, it has intertwined with broader bilateral tensions, including trade and tourism ties.

It's important to note that while the termination ends the specific 2001 framework, it does not immediately alter existing maritime activities. Fishing and limited exploration continue under status quo arrangements, but the lack of a formal agreement heightens risks of incidents at sea.

## Implications for Regional Relations and Resources

The scrapping of the deal could reshape Southeast Asian geopolitics. Thailand, a major economy in the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN), may seek support from regional partners to bolster its position. Cambodia, meanwhile, has hinted at pursuing legal avenues internationally, which could draw in other nations with similar South China Sea disputes.

Economically, the Gulf of Thailand's potential for oil and gas is significant. Estimates suggest billions of dollars in untapped reserves, but without resolution, joint ventures remain elusive. Thailand's state-owned **PTT Exploration and Production** has previously expressed interest in collaborative drilling, but the deadlock has delayed projects. This uncertainty might deter foreign investors, affecting energy security for both countries.

On a diplomatic level, the move underscores the challenges of resolving legacy border issues in the region. ASEAN's non-interference principle limits multilateral intervention, leaving bilateral channels as the primary recourse—channels that have proven ineffective here.

## What's Next in the Dispute

Looking ahead, Thailand's foreign ministry has indicated openness to new negotiations, possibly under a revised framework that addresses past shortcomings. Cambodia's response remains pending, but officials there have emphasized a desire for equitable resolution.

International observers suggest third-party mediation, perhaps through the UN or ASEAN, could restart talks. Without progress, the risk of resource nationalism increases, where each side might license unilateral exploration, potentially leading to confrontations.

For now, the termination of the 2001 deal resets the clock on what was already a stalled process. Stakeholders in both countries will watch closely, as the outcome could influence not just maritime boundaries but also broader cooperation in trade, security, and environmental management of the Gulf.

This development highlights the enduring complexities of territorial disputes in Asia, where economic incentives clash with national sovereignty. Travelers and businesses alike should stay informed, as ripple effects could emerge in unexpected ways.
