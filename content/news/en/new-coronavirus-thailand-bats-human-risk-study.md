A team of Japanese and Thai scientists, led by a virologist from the **University of Tokyo**, has identified a new coronavirus in bats in Thailand that is related to the virus causing COVID-19 and may be capable of infecting humans, according to a study reported on May 7, 2026. The discovery highlights ongoing risks of zoonotic diseases spilling over from wildlife to people in Southeast Asia, where bats are known reservoirs for such viruses. While no human infections have been confirmed yet, the findings underscore the need for continued surveillance in regions with high biodiversity and human-wildlife interactions.

### Background on the Discovery

The research, detailed in a report by [Nikkei Asia](https://asia.nikkei.com/business/science/new-coronavirus-found-in-thailand-may-be-able-to-infect-people-study), stems from fieldwork examining bat populations in Thailand. Bats have long been recognized as natural hosts for coronaviruses, including the SARS-CoV-2 strain responsible for the global COVID-19 pandemic that began in late 2019. This new variant was found during routine sampling efforts aimed at monitoring emerging pathogens in wildlife.

The study was conducted collaboratively between experts from the **University of Tokyo** and Thai institutions, reflecting strengthened international partnerships in virology research post-COVID. The virus shares genetic similarities with SARS-CoV-2, particularly in key regions that allow binding to human cells. However, the researchers emphasize that while laboratory analysis suggests potential for human infection—based on its ability to use receptors similar to those exploited by COVID-19—the evidence is preliminary and does not indicate active transmission to people.

Thailand, with its diverse ecosystems ranging from tropical forests to urban fringes, provides an ideal environment for such discoveries. Bats in the country, including fruit bats and insectivores, are abundant and often roost near human settlements. Previous outbreaks, such as Nipah virus incidents in nearby regions, have originated from similar wildlife interfaces. This finding adds to a growing body of evidence that Southeast Asia remains a hotspot for novel coronaviruses, with Thailand's proximity to bat habitats in Laos, Cambodia, and Myanmar amplifying regional concerns.

The announcement comes at a time when global health authorities are still recovering from the impacts of COVID-19, which affected Thailand's tourism-driven economy severely between 2020 and 2023. Although vaccination and public health measures have since normalized travel, vigilance against new threats persists. The study's lead from Tokyo underscores Japan's role in funding and expertise for tropical disease research, building on initiatives like those tracking avian influenza and other zoonoses.

### Implications for Public Health in Thailand

While the discovery is concerning, it is important to distinguish between potential risk and immediate danger. The virus was isolated solely from bats, with no reports of human cases linked to it as of the study's publication. Scientists involved note that "may be able to infect people" refers to in vitro (lab-based) tests showing compatibility with human cell receptors, but real-world transmission would require further adaptation, as seen with SARS-CoV-2's jump from animals to humans.

In Thailand, where ecotourism and agricultural activities bring people close to wildlife, this raises questions about exposure pathways. For instance, bat guano in caves or markets selling bushmeat could theoretically serve as bridges, though such practices are regulated. The Thai Ministry of Public Health has not yet issued specific alerts, but the study aligns with broader WHO recommendations for enhanced wildlife monitoring. Past experiences with COVID-19 taught lessons in rapid response, including contact tracing and genomic sequencing, which Thailand has bolstered through partnerships with international labs.

Comparatively, similar coronaviruses have been found in bats across Asia, from China to Indonesia, but few have crossed to humans. This Thai variant's relation to COVID-19 prompts comparisons: like its predecessor, it likely circulates asymptomatically in bats, posing spillover risks during environmental disruptions such as deforestation or climate shifts. However, experts caution against overreaction, as most bat coronaviruses remain animal-specific. The study's transparency helps in preempting panic, focusing instead on scientific preparedness.

For the scientific community, this underscores the value of ongoing surveillance programs. Thailand participates in global networks like the PREDICT project, which has identified hundreds of potential pathogens since 2009. The University of Tokyo's involvement highlights how cross-border research can detect threats early, potentially averting pandemics.

### What It Means for Travelers

Travelers to Thailand should remain aware of this development but need not alter plans drastically, as the risk to humans appears low based on current data. Popular destinations like Bangkok, Phuket, and Chiang Mai are far from primary bat habitats, though northern and southern forests host more wildlife. General precautions—avoiding contact with wild animals, not entering unmarked caves without guides, and practicing hand hygiene—align with standard travel advice for tropical regions.

For those planning eco-adventures, such as trekking in national parks, consulting updated health guidelines is advisable. Thailand's [practical info page](https://go2-thailand.com/practical-info/) covers vaccinations and insect repellents, which indirectly support zoonotic risk reduction. Visa requirements for short stays remain straightforward via [Thailand visa info](https://go2-thailand.com/visa/), unaffected by this news. International arrivals continue to face minimal COVID-era restrictions, with focus shifting to routine health checks.

In the event of broader alerts, Thailand's robust tourism infrastructure, including hospitals in major cities, ensures access to care. Visitors from regions with high bat exposure, like parts of Australia or Europe, might consider consulting travel clinics. Overall, this serves as a reminder of nature's unpredictability, encouraging responsible tourism that respects wildlife boundaries.

### What's Next for Research and Monitoring

The research team plans further studies to assess the virus's transmissibility, including animal models and expanded field sampling. Collaboration with Thai authorities could lead to targeted surveillance in high-risk areas, potentially integrating AI for genomic tracking. Globally, this may influence funding for bat research, with organizations like the WHO calling for unified Asian strategies.

As climate change expands wildlife ranges, proactive measures are crucial. Thailand's experience positions it well to lead regional responses, sharing data through ASEAN health forums. For now, the emphasis is on science over speculation, ensuring discoveries like this inform policy without fueling unfounded fears.
