# Train Driver Tests Positive for Drugs After Fatal Bangkok Bus Collision

The driver of a freight train involved in a catastrophic collision with a public bus in Bangkok has tested positive for drugs and been charged with reckless driving, Thai police announced. The crash at the **Asoke-Din Daeng railway crossing** on Saturday afternoon killed eight people and injured dozens more, reigniting safety concerns at one of the city's most dangerous transport intersections.

The train struck the bus after it became stuck on the railway tracks due to heavy traffic that prevented the crossing barriers from lowering properly. The bus burst into flames immediately after impact. According to the [BBC](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c3v2ngnpgl9o), authorities have not specified which drugs were detected in the train driver's urine test.

## What Happened at the Crossing

The Asoke-Din Daeng railway crossing is notorious for congestion and safety hazards. Initial investigations suggest the public bus had stalled on the tracks because traffic was so heavy that the manually-operated safety barrier could not be lowered in time. The crossing leads to one of Bangkok's busiest intersections, where an estimated **100,000 vehicles pass through daily** — well above safety thresholds for such crossings, according to Dr Amorn Phimarnmas, president of the Structural Engineers Association of Thailand.

Beyond the train driver, both the bus driver and the guard responsible for manually lowering the barrier have also been charged by police. Preliminary findings from the train's black box recorder showed that the emergency braking system was activated only about **100 metres (330 feet)** before the bus — insufficient distance to prevent collision, Thai PBS reported.

## A Pattern of Risk at the Crossing

The Asoke-Din Daeng crossing exemplifies a broader infrastructure problem in Bangkok. The railway tracks were built long before the surrounding roads were developed, and the city has grown around them in ways that create constant danger. Dr Amorn describes this as "risk normalisation" — when hazards become so routine that people assume "it'll be fine" and continue taking risks until disaster strikes.

Motorcyclists regularly weave through safety barriers to avoid traffic delays caused by passing trains. The crossing's hectic nature has made such behaviour commonplace, despite its obvious dangers.

## New Safety Measures

In response to the crash, **Pichet Kunadhamraks**, director-general of Thailand's rail transport department, has ordered mandatory drug and alcohol testing for all train drivers and railway staff before their shifts begin. This represents a significant tightening of safety protocols, though it remains unclear whether such measures will address the fundamental infrastructure challenges at problem crossings like Asoke-Din Daeng.

The incident highlights systemic vulnerabilities in Bangkok's transport network where aging railway infrastructure intersects with modern urban congestion. Experts suggest that longer-term solutions may require physical separation of rail and road traffic or significant traffic management improvements at high-risk crossings.

## What This Means for Visitors

While Bangkok's public transport system remains widely used by millions of residents and visitors daily, this incident underscores the importance of exercising caution at railway crossings throughout the city. Travellers should never attempt to cross railway tracks when barriers are lowering or when trains are approaching, and should allow extra time when navigating areas near railway intersections during peak traffic hours.
