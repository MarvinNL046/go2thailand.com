Thailand doesn't have a strong tipping culture — it's not expected the way it is in the United States. But service staff salaries are low, and tips for good service are genuinely appreciated. Here's exactly how much to leave, where, and when.

## The Quick Reference

| Service | Tip Amount | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| **Street food** | None | Not expected, not practical |
| **Casual restaurant** | Round up or 20-50 THB | Leave change on the table |
| **Mid-range restaurant** | 5-10% or 50-100 THB | Check if service charge included |
| **Upscale restaurant** | 10-15% | Usually has service charge already |
| **Thai massage (1 hr)** | 50-100 THB | Hand directly to therapist |
| **Spa treatment** | 10-15% of bill | Cash, directly to therapist |
| **Taxi (metered)** | Round up to nearest 10-20 THB | Not expected but appreciated |
| **Grab/Bolt** | None built into app | Optional cash tip if great service |
| **Hotel porter** | 20-50 THB per bag | Give when bags delivered to room |
| **Hotel housekeeping** | 20-50 THB/day | Leave on pillow or desk daily |
| **Tour guide (full day)** | 100-300 THB | Per person, end of tour |
| **Tour driver** | 50-100 THB | Separate from guide tip |
| **Diving instructor** | 200-500 THB | After multi-day course |
| **Cooking class instructor** | 100-200 THB | Optional but welcomed |

## Restaurant Tipping Explained

### Street Food and Markets
No tip expected — ever. Street vendors price their food to make a living, and the transaction is simple: you pay, you eat. Don't leave coins on the cart; it might confuse the vendor.

### Casual Thai Restaurants
The small, family-run places with plastic chairs and laminated menus. If your bill is 185 THB, leaving 200 THB and not taking change is a natural, low-key way to tip. Nobody will be offended if you don't; nobody will chase you for the 15 THB.

### Mid-Range and Tourist Restaurants
Check your bill first — many mid-range restaurants add a **7% VAT and 10% service charge** automatically. If you see "SC 10%" on the bill, a tip isn't necessary (though the service charge doesn't always reach the staff). If no service charge, leave 5-10% or 50-100 THB.

### Fine Dining
Upscale restaurants almost always include a service charge. If service was exceptional, an additional 100-200 THB in cash is a generous gesture.

## Massage and Spa Tipping

This is where tipping matters most in Thailand. [Massage therapists](https://go2-thailand.com/blog/thai-massage-guide-types-prices/) earn modest base salaries, and tips form a significant part of their income.

**Key rule: always tip in cash, directly to your therapist.** If you leave money at reception, it may not reach them.

| Treatment | Suggested Tip |
|-----------|--------------|
| Budget massage shop (200-300 THB/hr) | 50-100 THB |
| Mid-range spa (500-800 THB/hr) | 100-200 THB |
| Luxury resort spa (1,500+ THB/hr) | 200-500 THB |

## Taxi and Transport

Thai taxi drivers don't expect tips, but rounding up is standard practice:

- **Metered taxi**: If the meter reads 87 THB, hand over 100 THB and say "mai tong torn" (no need for change)
- **Grab/Bolt**: No tipping mechanism in the app. If a driver helps with luggage or navigates difficult traffic, 20-40 THB cash is kind
- **Tuk-tuk**: No tip expected — you already negotiated the fare
- **Long-distance private driver**: 100-200 THB for a full-day hire

## Hotel Tipping

- **Porter/bellboy**: 20-50 THB per bag when delivered to your room
- **Housekeeping**: 20-50 THB per day left on the pillow or desk (not the bathroom — it might be missed)
- **Concierge**: 50-100 THB if they've been particularly helpful with bookings or recommendations
- **Room service**: Check if service charge is included; if not, 20-50 THB

## When NOT to Tip

- **7-Eleven**: Never
- **Grab Food delivery**: Not expected (though you can add a tip in the app)
- **Government offices**: Never (could be misconstrued as a bribe)
- **Temple visits**: Donations go in the donation box, not to individual monks
- **Market vendors**: Prices are already set; just pay what's asked

## The Philosophy

Thailand's approach to tipping reflects its culture of **kreng jai** — consideration for others' feelings. Thais tip subtly and modestly. Flashy, generous tipping can actually make staff uncomfortable. A small, sincere gesture of appreciation is valued far more than a large, performative one.

When in doubt: round up, smile, and say "khop khun khrap/ka" (thank you). That combination covers 95% of tipping situations in Thailand.
