A 37-year-old backpacker from Edinburgh, **Rory McColl**, risks up to three years in a Thai jail after what he describes as a simple mistake: picking up the wrong mobile phone in a Bangkok bar on the first night of his holiday. The incident occurred on 9 March in a venue on the bustling Khao San Road, a famous nightlife area in [Bangkok](https://go2-thailand.com/city/bangkok/), turning his planned 12-day trip into a seven-week ordeal of detention and uncertainty. McColl was arrested shortly after, spent two nights in a concrete cell, and now awaits a preliminary court hearing on 28 April while confined to a hotel in [Pattaya](https://go2-thailand.com/city/pattaya/), unable to leave the country due to his confiscated passport.

## Background of the Incident

Rory McColl, a father from Scotland, had just arrived in Thailand after a 30-hour journey from Edinburgh. Eager to unwind, he headed to Khao San Road, known worldwide for its vibrant backpacker scene filled with bars, street food, and lively crowds. According to details shared with his family and reported by the [BBC](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c87wz7n5pwqo), McColl realized he had misplaced his own phone during drinks at the bar. Spotting what appeared to be an identical device on the counter, he picked it up and pocketed it without realizing it belonged to someone else—a woman nearby.

The misunderstanding escalated quickly when bar staff noticed and alerted the police. McColl was arrested on the spot, handcuffed, and taken into custody. His parents, **Helen** and **John McColl**, have been in daily contact via FaceTime and described the events to BBC Scotland News. They recounted how their son spent two nights in a stark concrete jail cell, using a plastic water bottle as a pillow and sleeping on the floor. "It all happened so quickly—before Rory knew it the police were there," Helen said, emphasizing the shock after such a long trip.

McColl explained the situation to authorities, claiming it was an honest error. He even communicated with the phone's owner, and the BBC reviewed an email exchange where she seemed to support his account. However, the BBC could not independently verify the emails' authenticity, and the woman has since stopped responding to McColl's messages. Despite this, Thai police are proceeding with charges, leaving McColl in limbo.

## The Arrest and Its Immediate Aftermath

Following his release on bail—requiring a payment of £1,000—McColl's passport was seized, preventing him from departing Thailand. For the past seven weeks, he has been staying in a hotel in Pattaya, a coastal resort town about 90 miles southeast of Bangkok, popular among tourists for its beaches and nightlife. His parents report that he rarely leaves the hotel, confined by the ongoing case and fear of further complications.

The conditions of his initial detention were particularly harrowing. McColl told his family the cell was "horrendous," with no comforts beyond the basics. After the second night, he was allowed out on bail but remained under strict restrictions. This has disrupted his life back home, where he is a father, and strained his family emotionally. Helen and John, speaking from Scotland, expressed being "worried sick" and doing everything possible to support him remotely.

Complicating matters further, while in custody, McColl was approached by a man claiming to be a lawyer offering court representation. Desperate for help, McColl paid the individual twice, hoping to resolve the issue and return to his son. However, his family grew suspicious when the man advised him to travel two hours to court only to send him away, citing a "bad judge" that day. No progress was made, and McColl's current lawyer has indicated this was likely a scam targeting vulnerable foreigners in legal trouble.

## Legal Concerns and Potential Scams for Travelers

This case highlights the risks of minor misunderstandings in Thailand's legal system, where even unintentional acts can lead to serious charges. McColl faces potential theft accusations, punishable by up to three years in prison if the preliminary hearing on 28 April does not drop the case. Thai law treats such incidents firmly, especially in tourist areas where quick arrests can occur to maintain order.

The suspected scam adds another layer of concern. As McColl's experience shows, individuals in distress may encounter opportunistic "fixers" preying on language barriers and unfamiliarity with local processes. His family's alarm bells rang when the man delayed action without results, a common tactic in reported tourist scams. While the BBC could not confirm all details, the pattern aligns with known issues for travelers in Thailand, underscoring the need for caution when seeking legal aid abroad. ## What's Next for Rory McColl

The upcoming court hearing on 28 April will be pivotal. If charges proceed, McColl could face trial and a lengthy sentence; if dropped, he may regain his passport and finally end this nightmare. His parents remain hopeful but anxious, maintaining daily FaceTime calls to keep his spirits up. McColl has expressed distress over missing time with his son and the toll of the uncertainty.
