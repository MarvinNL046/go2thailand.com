Thailand's cabinet has approved a significant reduction to its visa-free entry scheme, cutting the allowance from **60 days to 30 days** for tourists from more than 90 countries, according to [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/5/19/thailand-to-slash-tourist-visa-free-stays). The decision, announced on Tuesday, marks a sharp reversal from the generous exemption introduced in July 2024 to boost post-pandemic tourism recovery. The new tiered system will also reduce stays to just 15 days for citizens of some countries.

## Why Thailand Is Making the Change

Government spokesperson **Rachada Dhanadirek** told reporters in Bangkok that while tourism remains essential to the Thai economy, security concerns have taken priority. Thai officials acknowledged that the 60-day visa-free window inadvertently created loopholes that enabled illicit activities.

The policy reversal follows a series of high-profile arrests involving foreign nationals engaged in drug trafficking, human smuggling, and running unauthorised local businesses such as hotels and language schools. Officials noted that the generous scheme had allowed some people to exploit it, leading to a surge in grey-market enterprises, unauthorised foreign workers, and online scam operations. Policymakers now view a 30-day ceiling as sufficient to accommodate genuine, high-value travellers while tightening security.

## Who Is Affected

The previous 60-day visa exemption applied to nationals of approximately 93 countries, including the United States, Israel, parts of South America, and all 29 nations in Europe's Schengen zone. Under the new framework, most of these countries will see their visa-free stays capped at 30 days, while some will be reduced further to 15 days.

The exact breakdown of which countries fall into the 30-day versus 15-day categories has not been fully detailed in available reports, so travellers from affected nations should verify their specific entitlements with Thai immigration authorities or their nearest Thai embassy before planning trips.

## What This Means for Visitors

For many international tourists, the reduction from 60 to 30 days represents a meaningful change to travel planning. Those wishing to stay longer than 30 days will now need to apply for a tourist visa before arrival or explore other visa categories upon entry. The 30-day window remains sufficient for typical holiday visits, but extended stays—such as those for digital nomads, language students, or long-term explorers—will require alternative visa arrangements.

Visitors should note that the new tiered system introduces complexity: depending on nationality, some travellers may qualify for only 15 days visa-free. This makes it essential to check your country's specific allocation before booking accommodation or planning activities.

## What's Next

The cabinet's approval signals that Thailand is prioritising border security and preventing misuse of its tourism scheme over maximising visitor numbers. The government has not announced a specific implementation date for the new system, so travellers planning trips in the coming weeks should monitor official Thai immigration announcements for clarity on when the changes take effect.
