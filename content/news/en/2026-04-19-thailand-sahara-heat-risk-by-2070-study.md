# Thailand Risks Sahara-Like Heat by 2070, Climate Research Warns

BANGKOK — Thailand could experience extreme heat comparable to current conditions in the **Sahara** by **2070**, according to climate research highlighted by Tara Buakamsri, director of the Climate Connectors programme. The projections draw from a **2020** article by Owen Mulhern titled *Too Hot To Live: Climate Change In Thailand* and the influential **2020** study *Future Of The Human Climate Niche* by Xu and colleagues, warning that global warming is shifting temperatures beyond ranges where human societies have historically thrived.

## Background on the Research

The warning stems from discussions around established climate studies, as reported by [The Straits Times](https://www.straitstimes.com/asia/se-asia/thailand-could-become-as-hot-as-the-sahara-by-2070-research-shows). Tara Buakamsri referenced these works to underscore Thailand's vulnerability. The **2020** study by Xu and colleagues introduces the concept of a "human climate niche," a narrow band of temperatures where human populations have predominantly evolved and settled over millennia.

At the time of the referenced research, **19** of the **20** hottest years on record had occurred since **2001**, illustrating the accelerating trend of global warming. A photo accompanying the report shows a Thai man shielding himself from the sun in [Bangkok](https://go2-thailand.com/city/bangkok/) on **March 30, 2026**, capturing the intensifying heat already affecting daily life in the capital.

This body of research does not predict exact daily temperatures but focuses on long-term mean annual averages, projecting that parts of Thailand could approach levels currently rare on Earth.

## Understanding the Human Climate Niche

The core idea is that human civilisations have clustered around a mean annual temperature of roughly **11°C to 15°C**. Many populations today live in warmer areas but still within adaptable limits. The study identifies **29°C** as a critical threshold: conditions above this are presently found on only about **0.8 per cent** of the Earth's land surface, primarily in the **Sahara** Desert.

Pushing beyond this niche could challenge human habitability, as societies have limited historical experience adapting to such sustained heat. The research argues that rising temperatures from climate change threaten to shift regions like Thailand into this extreme category by **2070**, though it emphasises trends rather than certainties.

For context, Thailand's current climate already features hot seasons, but the projection signals a potential escalation to Sahara-like means, where extreme aridity and heat dominate.

## Implications for Thailand

Thailand, with its tropical climate, faces heightened risks as outlined in Mulhern's **2020** article. Urban areas like [Bangkok](https://go2-thailand.com/city/bangkok/) and rural regions alike could see prolonged heat stress, impacting agriculture, health, and infrastructure. The Straits Times article notes that such changes could test the limits of human adaptability, especially as global trends continue.

While the research is from **2020**, its relevance persists amid ongoing warming. Recent records, like the **2026** heat in Bangkok, align with the broader pattern of intensifying extremes. No specific mitigation measures for Thailand are detailed in the source, but the discussion highlights the need for awareness of these long-term shifts.

## What This Means for Visitors

Travelers to Thailand should prepare for hotter conditions, even in the near term. Current advice includes staying hydrated, avoiding midday sun, and using sun protection, as seen in everyday scenes from [Bangkok](https://go2-thailand.com/city/bangkok/). For longer-term plans, monitor updates via [practical info resources](https://go2-thailand.com/practical-info/) on heat safety and seasonal weather.

The projections to **2070** are distant but underscore the value of sustainable travel practices today.

Ongoing climate monitoring will refine these forecasts. Researchers like those behind the Xu study continue tracking global niches, while programmes like Climate Connectors advocate for action.
