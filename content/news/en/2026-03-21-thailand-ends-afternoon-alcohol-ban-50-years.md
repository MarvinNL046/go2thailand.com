For the first time in over **50 years**, Thailand has officially ended its ban on alcohol sales between **2:00 PM and 5:00 PM**. Registered stores can now sell alcohol continuously from **11:00 AM to midnight** — eliminating one of the most confusing rules travelers encountered in the country.

## What Changed

For decades, Thailand enforced a strict two-window alcohol sales policy:

- **Old rules**: Alcohol sales allowed 11:00 AM - 2:00 PM, then banned until 5:00 PM, then allowed again 5:00 PM - midnight
- **New rules**: Continuous sales from **11:00 AM to midnight** at registered establishments

The afternoon gap caught countless tourists off guard — attempting to buy a beer at 3 PM at 7-Eleven, only to be told by the cashier that sales were blocked until 5 PM. That experience is now history.

According to [Siam Legal](https://siam-legal.com/travel-to-thailand/thailand-lifts-afternoon-alcohol-sales-ban-ahead-of-new-year-celebrations/), the change was implemented to modernize Thailand's approach to alcohol regulation while supporting the tourism and hospitality industries.

## What Hasn't Changed

Several alcohol rules remain firmly in place:

### No Sales Before 11 AM
You still can't buy alcohol before 11:00 AM anywhere in Thailand. Morning beer runs remain off-limits.

### Buddhist Holiday "Dry Days"
Thailand observes several **alcohol-free days** each year when sales are completely banned — including in restaurants and bars. For 2026, according to [ThaiRanked](https://thairanked.com/en/blogs/no-alcohol-days-thailand-2026/):

| Date | Holiday | Alcohol Ban |
|------|---------|------------|
| March 3 | Makha Bucha Day | All day |
| May 11 | Visakha Bucha Day | All day |
| July 9 | Asanha Bucha Day | All day |
| July 10 | Buddhist Lent (Khao Phansa) | All day |
| October 6 | End of Buddhist Lent | All day |

On these days, shops, bars, and restaurants are prohibited from selling alcohol. Some hotels may still serve to registered guests in private settings, but don't count on it. Plan accordingly if your trip coincides with these dates.

### Songkran Alcohol Zones
During [Songkran](https://go2-thailand.com/news/2026-03-20-bangkok-songkran-2026-khao-san-road-safety-plan/) (April 13-15), many designated celebration areas enforce **"White Songkran" zones** where alcohol is banned within the festival perimeter. This includes popular spots like Khao San Road and Silom Road in Bangkok.

### Election Days
Alcohol sales are banned from **6 PM the day before** until **midnight on election day**. With Thailand's recent [February elections](https://go2-thailand.com/news/2026-03-20-anutin-reelected-thailand-prime-minister/), this rule recently affected travelers.

## Other Alcohol Rules Travelers Should Know

- **Drinking age**: 20 years old (not 18 or 21)
- **ID checks**: Increasingly common at 7-Eleven, supermarkets, and bars — carry your passport or a photo of it
- **Beach drinking**: Generally tolerated but technically prohibited in some national park areas
- **Drunk driving**: Strict enforcement, especially during [Songkran's "Seven Dangerous Days"](https://go2-thailand.com/news/2026-03-21-songkran-road-safety-dangerous-days-what-travelers-must-know/) — 24/7 DUI checkpoints with vehicle seizure
- **Public intoxication**: Can result in fines, particularly in festival areas

## Practical Impact for Travelers

The end of the afternoon ban mostly affects casual daytime drinking:

- **Beach bars**: Can now serve drinks continuously through the afternoon — no more 3 PM cutoff
- **Restaurant lunches**: A beer or wine with a late lunch is no longer a timed operation
- **Hotel minibars**: Were already exempt from the ban, but surrounding shops now match
- **7-Eleven**: The checkout system no longer blocks alcohol purchases between 2-5 PM

For nightlife-focused travelers, nothing changes — bars and clubs already operated on evening hours. The reform primarily benefits daytime leisure and dining.
