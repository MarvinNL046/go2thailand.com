Thailand's government is set to propose reducing visa-free stays from 60 days to 30 days for tourists from 93 countries, driven by escalating concerns over foreign nationals engaging in illegal business operations and other misconduct. The move, announced by Foreign Affairs Minister **Sihasak Phuangketkaeow** on Tuesday, May 12, 2026, targets a broader review of visa policies amid local backlash in popular tourist areas. Officials aim to curb abuses while preserving the tourism sector, which powers much of the economy, as reported by [The Independent](https://www.independent.co.uk/travel/news-and-advice/thailand-visa-free-restrictions-tourists-russia-china-b2975555.html).

## Background on the Controversy

Thailand's tourism industry has long been a cornerstone of its economy, attracting nearly 40 million visitors in 2019 before the Covid-19 pandemic halted global travel. In recent years, visitor numbers have rebounded strongly, partly fueled by people fleeing conflicts such as the Russia-Ukraine war and the war in Gaza. This influx has revitalized hotspots like Bangkok, Phuket, and Chiang Mai, but it has also sparked significant local resentment.

Residents in these areas complain that foreigners, particularly Russians and Chinese expatriates, are increasingly involved in illegal activities. A key issue is the operation of businesses without proper authorization, often achieved by registering companies under Thai nominees to bypass strict foreign ownership laws. These practices are seen as undercutting local workers, driving up prices for housing and services, and transforming communities into enclaves dominated by outsiders.

The pressure on authorities has mounted, with allegations of criminal behavior adding to the tension. In tourist-heavy regions, reports of foreigners intimidating locals or running unregulated enterprises on public spaces like beaches have fueled calls for stricter controls. This controversy highlights the challenges of Thailand's liberal visa policies, which were designed to boost arrivals but now face scrutiny for enabling exploitation.

## Details of the Proposed Changes

The core of the proposal involves halving the visa-free stay period from 60 days to 30 days for nationals of 93 countries currently eligible for such entry. Minister **Sihasak Phuangketkaeow** plans to submit this to the cabinet for approval, emphasizing a comprehensive overhaul. Beyond the visa-free adjustment, the review will examine eligibility for other categories, including investment visas, long-term residency permits, student visas, and digital nomad schemes.

These changes aim to close loopholes that allow extended stays under false pretenses, such as using tourist visas for work or business. While the exact list of the 93 countries has not been specified in available details, the policy targets those with high volumes of overstays or reported violations. Officials stress that the goal is not to deter genuine tourists but to ensure compliance with labor and commerce regulations.

This builds on existing efforts to enforce rules more rigorously. For instance, during a recent visit to Phuket, Prime Minister **Anutin Charnvirakul** warned of zero tolerance for foreign "thugs" operating illegally on public beaches or harassing residents. The prime minister's statements underscore a shift toward balancing openness with enforcement.

## Government Response and Ongoing Actions

Prime Minister **Anutin Charnvirakul** has positioned the government as committed to maintaining visa-free access, recognizing tourism's vital role in revenue generation. On Monday, May 11, 2026, he stated, “Free visas are not about unconditional entry. We need to find the best balance.” This reflects a nuanced approach: preserving economic benefits while addressing community grievances.

To implement crackdowns, the prime minister is scheduled to visit **Koh Phangan**, renowned for its Full Moon Party at Haad Rin beach, white-sand shores, and vibrant nightlife. The trip will focus on inspecting businesses run by foreigners but registered as Thai-owned, alongside enforcing tourism and labor laws. Agencies involved include security forces, administrative bodies, the commerce ministry, and others, as instructed by the prime minister.

These actions follow similar initiatives in other areas. In Phuket, inspections targeted illegal operations, signaling a nationwide push. While no timeline for cabinet approval has been given, the proposal's submission indicates momentum toward implementation in the coming months.

## What This Means for Travelers

For visitors from the affected 93 countries, the reduction to 30 days could significantly impact trip planning, especially for those seeking longer explorations of Thailand's diverse regions—from the bustling streets of Bangkok to the serene islands in the Andaman Sea. Short-term tourists may face fewer disruptions, but digital nomads, retirees, or business travelers relying on extended stays will need to explore alternatives like proper work visas or extensions.

The review of other visa types suggests potential tightening, so applicants should prepare for stricter scrutiny on intentions and documentation. While speculation exists about broader restrictions, officials have clarified that the policy aims to target abusers, not penalize all foreigners. Travelers are advised to stay updated via official channels and comply with local laws to avoid issues.
