Thailand's fuel distribution crisis continues to deepen, with new data revealing the staggering scale of panic buying — and neighboring Malaysia now restricting fuel sales to Thai vehicles crossing the border.

## Panic Buying by the Numbers

According to government data reported by [ThailandBlog.nl](https://www.thailandblog.nl/nieuws-uit-thailand/thailand-nieuwsupdate-algemeen-18-maart-2026/), daily diesel sales in Thailand have surged **85%** — from normal levels to approximately **118 million liters per day**. This massive spike, driven entirely by panic buying rather than actual increased demand, has overwhelmed the distribution network connecting refineries to retail stations.

The government maintains that Thailand holds **101 days of fuel reserves** at the national level. The problem remains distribution, not supply — but that distinction matters little to the driver staring at an empty pump.

## Diesel Price Rises Again

On March 18, diesel prices rose another **0.50 baht per liter**, though the government is capping the retail price under **33 baht per liter**. This price cap is straining the government's Oil Fund, which now faces a deficit of **12.6 billion baht**.

The pricing squeeze creates a difficult dynamic: the cap keeps prices artificially low, which actually encourages more consumption and hoarding. But removing the cap would cause an immediate price shock that could further destabilize the economy.

## Malaysia Restricts Fuel to Thai Vehicles

In a telling cross-border development, gas stations in Malaysia's **Kedah and Perlis provinces** (the northern border area near Thailand) have capped fuel sales to Thai-registered vehicles at just **20 liters per fill-up**.

The restriction was triggered by a surge in Thai drivers crossing into Malaysia to take advantage of Malaysia's lower, subsidized fuel prices. The measure is described as temporary, but reflects how Thailand's domestic fuel crisis is spilling across borders.

**For travelers planning border crossings to Malaysia:** Don't count on filling up on the Malaysian side. Ensure you have enough fuel to reach your destination within Thailand before crossing.

## Tourism Sector Seeks Emergency Relief

Thailand's tourism industry is feeling the squeeze. Higher oil prices are pushing up costs across the board:

- **Flight prices**: domestic and regional airfares under upward pressure
- **Ground transport**: tour buses, airport transfers, and Grab/Bolt rides affected
- **Accommodation**: some properties may pass energy costs to guests

Industry groups are lobbying the government for emergency support, including possible excise duty reductions and promotional flight deals for international visitors — such as complimentary domestic return flights bundled with international ticket purchases.

## Bangkok Air Quality Also a Concern

Meanwhile, Bangkok's PM2.5 air quality readings are averaging **32 µg/m³** — just under the Thai standard. Twelve districts have exceeded safe limits at **42 µg/m³**, and heat index values are topping **36°C**.

Travelers in Bangkok should check air quality apps and consider wearing masks on high-pollution days, particularly during morning hours.
