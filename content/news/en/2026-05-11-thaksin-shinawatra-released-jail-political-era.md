Former Thai Prime Minister **Thaksin Shinawatra**, a polarizing figure in the country's politics, was released from Bangkok's Klong Prem Central Prison on Monday after serving part of a one-year sentence for corruption and abuses of power during his tenure from 2001 to 2006. At age 76, Thaksin emerged to cheers from hundreds of red-clad supporters, marking a significant moment in Thailand's turbulent political landscape, where his influence has persisted despite years of exile and legal battles.

## Thaksin's Long Shadow Over Thai Politics

Thaksin Shinawatra's release underscores his enduring impact on Thailand, even after spending most of the past two decades in exile and the last eight months behind bars. A self-made billionaire who swept to power in January 2001, Thaksin quickly became a transformative yet divisive leader. His brash style and policies aimed at reshaping the nation won him devoted followers, particularly among rural and working-class voters, but also fierce opposition from the royalist establishment and urban elites.

Throughout his career, Thaksin's parties have repeatedly triumphed in elections, defying attempts to sideline him. He was ousted in a military coup in September 2006, yet his political machine endured. This led to a cycle of court rulings against his allies, violent street protests, and another coup in 2014. Undeterred, Thaksin directed his Pheu Thai party from abroad, maintaining a grip on power. In 2023, following what observers described as a "grand bargain" with conservative opponents, he returned to Thailand, positioning Pheu Thai to lead a coalition government once more.

His popularity remains evident. Outside the prison, supporters gathered in force. One fan, Maysa Lombuarot, traveled 700km to witness the event, bringing 20kg of lychees because "I know he likes them." She expressed hope that Thaksin would resume his political role to "help the country" and fulfill past promises, highlighting the loyalty that has sustained his influence.

Thaksin appeared in a white shirt with short-cropped hair, telling reporters he was in good health and felt "relieved." He was greeted by family, including his daughter Paetongtarn Shinawatra, a former prime minister and his political protégé. Despite Pheu Thai's assurances that Thaksin will now step back—focusing on family and grandchildren—media speculation swirls about his future involvement, given his history of defying retirement.

## Recent Turbulence and the Path to Release

Thaksin's imprisonment began last September, following a Supreme Court ruling that his six-month stay in a police hospital after returning to Thailand in 2023 was a deliberate evasion of his sentence. This verdict came amid broader political upheaval: less than two weeks prior, the Pheu Thai-led coalition collapsed when the Constitutional Court dismissed Paetongtarn as prime minister. The dismissal stemmed from a leaked phone conversation with Cambodian leader Hun Sen regarding a border dispute, reigniting tensions between the two nations.

These events reflect a pattern where Thailand's conservative courts, often aligned with royalist interests, have repeatedly shaped the fate of Thaksin's allies. While Thaksin was incarcerated, Pheu Thai suffered its worst electoral performance in the February general election. The party finished third, overshadowed by the reformist People's Party and the conservative Bhumjaithai, which capitalized on a surge of nationalist sentiment following a border war with Cambodia. As a result, Pheu Thai has been relegated to a junior role in the new coalition government.

The source of this reporting, [BBC](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c99l1590kr0o), notes that Thaksin's emergence from prison occurs against this backdrop of weakened influence, raising questions about whether his era of dominance has finally waned.

## Implications for Thailand's Political Stability

Thaksin's release could signal either a quiet exit or a resurgence, though the latter seems tempered by recent setbacks. His inability to fully retreat from politics—evident in his past remote leadership—suggests he may continue advising Pheu Thai informally. However, the party's diminished status and the rise of newer forces like the People's Party indicate shifting dynamics. The border conflict with Cambodia, which boosted nationalists, adds another layer of uncertainty, potentially fueling further divisions.

Speculation abounds, but facts point to a more subdued role. Pheu Thai's insistence on his background status aligns with the realities of court oversight and electoral losses. Yet, Thaksin's history of resilience means any prediction of his "era" ending remains speculative. The conservative establishment's wariness of his ambition persists, as seen in the multiple interventions against his family and allies.
