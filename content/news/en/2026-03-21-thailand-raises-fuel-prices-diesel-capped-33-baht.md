The Thai government began raising fuel prices on **March 19, 2026**, but is keeping diesel — the fuel that matters most for transport and consumer goods — capped at **33 baht per liter** through continued subsidies.

This is the fifth article in our coverage of [Thailand's ongoing fuel situation](https://go2-thailand.com/news/2026-03-21-thailand-fuel-crisis-update-diesel-85-percent-surge/).

## What Changed

Vice Premier Phiphat Ratchakitprakarn confirmed that diesel prices rose by **less than 1 baht per liter** on Wednesday, with the retail price capped at 33 baht. The increases are being implemented gradually rather than in a single jump, to avoid sudden shocks to transport and food costs.

Gasoline (benzine) prices are also rising, though specific amounts vary by grade.

## Why Diesel Gets Special Treatment

The government's reasoning is straightforward: diesel drives Thailand's economy. Higher diesel prices immediately cascade into:

- **Bus, truck, and freight costs** — affecting everything from online deliveries to fresh food distribution
- **Agricultural operations** — tractors, irrigation pumps, and harvest equipment all run on diesel
- **Consumer goods prices** — most products travel by diesel-powered truck at some point

By keeping diesel below 33 baht, the government aims to contain inflation at the consumer level even as global oil prices rise.

## B20 Biofuel Diesel: The Budget Option

An interesting development: **B20 diesel** (diesel with a higher biodiesel/palm oil content) remains available at **4-5 baht per liter less** than standard B7 diesel. The government is specifically subsidizing B20 for agriculture, industry, and transport sectors.

For travelers, B20 is sometimes available at gas stations — it works in most diesel vehicles with no modifications needed. If you're renting a diesel car or van, look for the B20 pump to save a few baht per liter.

## The Oil Fund Deficit

These subsidies don't come free. The government's Oil Fuel Fund — which absorbs the difference between market prices and retail caps — currently faces a deficit of approximately **12.6 billion baht**. The gradual price increases are partly designed to reduce this deficit without eliminating subsidies entirely.

It's a balancing act: too much subsidy drains state finances; too little sends transport costs — and therefore food and goods prices — spiraling upward.

## What This Means for Travelers

The fuel price changes have minimal direct impact on most tourists:

- **Grab and Bolt fares**: may edge slightly higher but both platforms have [driver support programs](https://go2-thailand.com/news/2026-03-20-grab-bolt-support-drivers-bangkok-oil-prices/) absorbing some costs
- **Domestic flights**: airlines may adjust fuel surcharges, but competitive pressure limits increases
- **BTS/MRT/buses**: urban public transport is electric or fixed-fare, unaffected by diesel prices
- **Rental vehicles**: if renting diesel, expect ~33 THB/liter. Look for B20 pumps to save 4-5 THB/liter
- **Tour prices**: some operators may add small fuel surcharges for boat trips and long-distance tours

The bigger concern remains [fuel availability](https://go2-thailand.com/news/2026-03-20-thailand-fuel-shortages-gas-stations/) at stations, which is a distribution problem separate from pricing.
