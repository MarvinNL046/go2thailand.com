Rising oil prices driven by Middle East tensions are squeezing ride-hail drivers across Bangkok, prompting both **Grab** and **Bolt** to announce support measures aimed at keeping drivers on the road — and fares affordable for passengers.

## Grab: 10 Million Baht in Direct Fuel Subsidies

Grab, Southeast Asia's largest ride-hailing platform, has announced a **10 million baht fuel subsidy program** for its Bangkok drivers and delivery riders. The temporary scheme runs from **March 18 to April 1, 2026**, with payments automatically deposited into drivers' e-wallets by April 6.

The subsidy amount varies by worker category — car drivers and motorcycle riders receive different levels of support — though Grab has not disclosed the exact per-driver breakdown.

The move comes as fuel costs eat into driver earnings. With diesel prices recently rising from 30 to 33 baht per liter amid [nationwide fuel distribution issues](https://go2-thailand.com/news/2026-03-20-thailand-fuel-shortages-gas-stations/), many drivers have reported that a growing share of their income goes straight to the pump.

## Bolt: Peak-Hour Bonus Adjustments

Bolt, Grab's main competitor in Bangkok, is taking a different approach. Rather than direct subsidies, the Estonian-founded platform is **restructuring its bonus system** to improve driver earnings during peak demand hours.

The idea: by helping drivers complete more trips when surge pricing is active, they can offset higher fuel costs through increased volume and better per-trip earnings. Bolt has not disclosed specific numbers.

## What This Means for Travelers

For tourists and expats who rely on Grab and Bolt for daily transport in Bangkok, the key question is whether ride fares will go up.

Both platforms face a delicate balancing act. As [ThailandBlog.nl](https://www.thailandblog.nl/nieuws-uit-thailand/grab-en-bolt-steunen-chauffeurs-in-bangkok-na-olieschok/) notes: if rides become too expensive, passengers switch to the BTS Skytrain or traditional taxis. But if driver earnings drop too far, drivers leave the platform — leading to longer wait times and reduced availability.

### Practical Tips for Travelers

- **Expect slightly longer wait times** during peak hours as some drivers reduce shifts to save fuel
- **Compare Grab and Bolt prices** before booking — competition between the two platforms helps keep fares competitive
- **Consider the BTS/MRT** for longer distances in Bangkok — fixed fares (17–65 baht) are unaffected by fuel prices
- **Tipping drivers** is appreciated, especially during this period of rising costs — even 20–40 baht makes a difference
- **Book during off-peak hours** (mid-morning, early afternoon) for better availability and lower surge pricing

The situation remains fluid. If Middle East tensions ease and fuel prices stabilize, both platforms will likely scale back their support programs. For now, travelers can expect ride-hailing to remain available and reasonably priced in Bangkok, though margins are tighter than usual.
