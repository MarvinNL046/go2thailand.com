Northern Thailand's annual burning season is in full swing, and **Chiang Mai's air quality has reached unhealthy levels** — with the city ranking among the world's 10 most polluted on some days in March 2026.

## How Bad Is It?

According to [IQAir](https://www.iqair.com/th-en/newsroom/chiang-mai-ranks-among-the-top-10-most-polluted-cities-during-thailand-s-burning-season-3-4-2026), Chiang Mai hit an AQI (Air Quality Index) exceeding **150** in early March, with PM2.5 as the dominant pollutant. On March 4, the city ranked **3rd among the most polluted major cities in the world**.

Air quality has fluctuated since then — dropping to moderate levels (AQI ~93) on some days before spiking again when wind conditions trap smoke in the mountain valley. The worst readings have exceeded **AQI 200** (classified as "Very Unhealthy"), particularly during still, windless mornings.

## What Causes It?

The burning season results from:

- **Agricultural clearing**: After harvest, farmers across northern Thailand and neighboring countries burn fields to prepare for the next planting season
- **Forest fires**: Both intentional (to promote new growth) and accidental fires in the surrounding mountains
- **Geography**: Chiang Mai sits in a valley surrounded by mountains, which trap smoke and particulate matter when there's no wind

The worst period typically runs from **late February through March**, sometimes extending into April. By May, the arrival of the rainy season clears the air.

## Health Risks

PM2.5 particles are small enough to penetrate deep into the lungs and enter the bloodstream. At the levels currently seen in Chiang Mai, health authorities warn of:

- Increased risk of **respiratory issues** (coughing, shortness of breath)
- Worsened **heart problems** for those with pre-existing conditions
- **Eye and throat irritation**
- Greater risks for **children, the elderly, and people with asthma**

## What Travelers Should Do

### If You're Already in Chiang Mai

- **Check real-time AQI** before going outdoors: [aqicn.org/city/chiang-mai](https://aqicn.org/city/chiang-mai/) or [IQAir Chiang Mai](https://www.iqair.com/us/thailand/chiang-mai/chiang-mai)
- **Wear an N95 or KN95 mask** outdoors when AQI exceeds 100 — available at pharmacies and 7-Eleven
- **Stay indoors during peak pollution hours** (6–10 AM and late evening when inversions trap smoke)
- **Use air-conditioned spaces** — most malls, cafes, and co-working spaces have filtered air
- **Run an air purifier** in your accommodation if available (many guesthouses now provide them during burning season)
- **Stay hydrated** and avoid intense outdoor exercise

### If You're Planning a Trip

- **Consider postponing Chiang Mai visits** until late April or May when rains clear the air
- **Alternative northern destinations**: Chiang Rai and Pai also experience burning season pollution
- **Unaffected destinations**: [Bangkok](https://go2-thailand.com/city/bangkok/), southern Thailand ([Phuket](https://go2-thailand.com/islands/phuket/), [Koh Samui](https://go2-thailand.com/islands/koh-samui/), [Krabi](https://go2-thailand.com/city/krabi/)), and eastern islands are not affected by northern burning season
- **Best months for Chiang Mai**: November through early February offers excellent air quality and cool weather

## Government Response

[Chiang Rai province has launched a "Fresh Air Operation"](https://go2-thailand.com/news/2026-03-21-thailand-weekly-roundup-diesel-weather-jobs/) deploying 15 water trucks across 18 districts, with agricultural burning banned through May 10. Chiang Mai authorities have implemented similar measures, though enforcement remains a challenge across the vast agricultural areas of northern Thailand.

The problem extends beyond Thailand — agricultural burning in neighboring Myanmar and Laos contributes significantly to the transboundary haze affecting the entire northern region.
