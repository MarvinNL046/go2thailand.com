Thailand's biggest airlines are raising the alarm about a threat that could derail the country's ambitious tourism targets for 2026: skyrocketing jet fuel prices driven by ongoing Middle East tensions.

## Fuel Prices Have Nearly Doubled

Jet A-1 fuel prices in Singapore — the benchmark for Southeast Asian carriers — have surged dramatically, climbing from around USD 80 per barrel before the Middle East conflict escalated to between USD 170 and USD 220 per barrel. That near-100 per cent increase has sent shockwaves through the aviation industry across the region.

Thai Airways, Bangkok Airways, Thai AirAsia and Nok Air have all publicly warned that these rising costs pose a serious risk to Thailand's tourism and hospitality sectors. Fuel typically accounts for 30 to 40 per cent of an airline's operating costs, making it the single largest expense for most carriers.

## Fares Are Climbing, Routes Are Shifting

Thai Airways has already lifted international fares by 10 to 15 per cent, with long-haul routes hit hardest. Flights to and from Europe are particularly affected because longer distances mean higher fuel burn, and many routes have been rerouted to avoid sensitive airspace in the Middle East, adding both time and cost.

The impact varies by market. Return tickets from Malaysia to Bangkok have climbed 12 to 15 per cent from February 2026 baselines, while carriers serving secondary Chinese cities now use dynamic pricing algorithms that push fares 20 to 25 per cent higher during peak weekend periods.

Late-night flights departing between 9 p.m. and midnight have also been reduced by 22 per cent since the start of March 2026, as airlines consolidate schedules to improve fuel efficiency.

## Industry Pushes for Government Help

Thai carriers are lobbying for jet fuel tax relief to ease the burden. Without government intervention, airlines warn that higher fares could discourage price-sensitive travellers — particularly from key source markets in Asia — from choosing Thailand over cheaper regional competitors.

## What It Means for Travellers

Despite the fare increases, advance bookings for the spring and summer season remain strong. Direct Europe-to-Thailand routes are reporting cabin load factors of 80 to 90 per cent, suggesting that demand remains robust even at higher prices.

Still, budget-conscious travellers heading to Thailand in 2026 should consider several strategies:

- **Book early** to lock in current fares before further increases.
- **Be flexible with dates**, as midweek flights tend to be cheaper than weekend departures.
- **Consider indirect routes** via hubs outside the Middle East, such as Singapore or Kuala Lumpur, which may offer better pricing.

The coming months will be critical in determining whether Thailand can maintain its tourism momentum or whether rising travel costs will put a brake on the boom.
