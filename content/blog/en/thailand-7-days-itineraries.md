## 1. The Classic Route: Bangkok → Ayutthaya → Chiang Mai (Culture & History)

This itinerary is perfect for first-time visitors who want a taste of Thailand's soul—ancient temples, bustling markets, and mountain culture all in one week.

### Why This Route?

The Bangkok-to-Chiang Mai corridor is Thailand's cultural spine. You'll move from the chaotic energy of the capital through the historical ruins of the ancient kingdom, ending in the serene mountains of the north. When we spent time exploring this route, we found it hits the sweet spot between iconic sights and authentic experiences—you're never too far from a train or bus, and the pace feels natural rather than rushed.

### Day-by-Day Breakdown

**Days 1–2: Bangkok**
Arrive and spend your first full day exploring [Bangkok's attractions](https://go2-thailand.com/city/bangkok/attractions/). Visit the Grand Palace (morning, arrive by 8:30 AM to beat crowds—entry ₿500), Wat Phra Kaew, and take a long-tail boat through the floating markets. Day 2: explore [Bangkok food](https://go2-thailand.com/city/bangkok/food/) at street markets, visit Jim Thompson House, and experience the chaos of Chatuchak Weekend Market if you're there Saturday–Sunday.

**Days 3–4: Ayutthaya**
Take a morning [train from](https://trip.tpo.lv/gNIdNBmi) Bangkok to Ayutthaya (1.5 hours, ₿15–50 depending on class). Spend two days exploring temple ruins by bicycle or scooter—Wat Mahathat, Wat Phra Si Sanphet, and Wat Chaiwatthanaram are unmissable. Stay overnight to experience the town without day-trippers. Local guides offer half-day tours for ₿300–500 per person.

**Days 5–7: Chiang Mai**
Overnight train from Ayutthaya to [Chiang Mai](https://go2-thailand.com/city/chiang-mai/) (12 hours, ₿600–1,500 depending on sleeper class). Arrive early morning. Day 5: visit Wat Chedi Luang and Wat Phra Singh in the Old City. Day 6: take a half-day cooking class (₿800–1,200) or visit an ethical elephant sanctuary. Day 7: explore the Sunday Walking Street market (if you're there Sunday evening) or visit local artisan workshops.

### Pro Tips for This Route

- **Book sleeper trains early:** The overnight Bangkok–Chiang Mai train books out 2–3 weeks in advance during peak season (Nov–Feb). Reserve at railway.co.th or through your hotel.
- **Skip the tuk-tuk scams:** In Ayutthaya, hire a bicycle (₿50–100/day) or book a guide through your hotel. Tuk-tuk drivers often push gem shops and overcharge tourists.
- **Cooking class timing:** Book your [Chiang Mai cooking class](https://go2-thailand.com/blog/thai-cooking-classes-chiang-mai/) the day before—most require advance notice.
- **Temple etiquette:** Wear shoulders and knees covered. Remove shoes before entering. Women should not touch monks.

## 2. Island Escape: Bangkok → Phuket → Koh Phi Phi → Koh Lanta (Beaches & Diving)

If you're dreaming of turquoise water, white sand, and coral reefs, this route delivers pure tropical bliss without sacrificing culture entirely.

### Why This Route?

The southern islands offer everything: snorkeling, diving, sunset cocktails, and enough beach time to actually relax. Unlike the overcrowded Koh Samui circuit, this route balances popular islands with quieter alternatives. We've found that Koh Phi Phi gives you that postcard-perfect island experience, while Koh Lanta offers a slower pace—the best of both worlds in seven days.

### Day-by-Day Breakdown

**Days 1–2: Bangkok**
Same as the classic route—spend one full day exploring the city, then a night flight or early morning flight to Phuket.

**Days 3–4: Phuket**
Fly Bangkok to Phuket (2 hours, ₿1,200–2,000). Skip Patong Beach if you want authenticity—instead, stay in Kamala or Karon for a quieter vibe. Day 3: relax on the beach, explore local markets. Day 4: take a snorkeling or diving trip to nearby islands (half-day tours ₿1,000–1,500) or visit [Phuket's Old Town](https://go2-thailand.com/islands/phuket/) for Sino-Portuguese architecture and street food.

**Days 5–6: Koh Phi Phi**
[Ferry](https://12go.tpo.lv/tNA80urD) from Phuket to [Koh Phi Phi](https://go2-thailand.com/islands/koh-phi-phi/) (1.5 hours, ₿300–400). Two days here: snorkel in Maya Bay, visit Monkey Beach, or take a full-day island-hopping tour (₿1,200–1,800). The island is touristy but stunning—arrive early to avoid crowds.

**Day 7: Koh Lanta**
Ferry from Koh Phi Phi to [Koh Lanta](https://go2-thailand.com/islands/koh-lanta/) (2–3 hours with connections, ₿400–600). Spend your final day on this laid-back island—quieter beaches, excellent seafood, and a slower pace. If you have time, visit the Old Town or take a sunset kayaking trip.

### Best Beaches & Snorkeling Spots

- **Maya Bay (Koh Phi Phi):** Iconic but crowded. Go early (before 10 AM) or skip it entirely for quieter spots.
- **Bamboo Island:** Day trip from Koh Phi Phi. Pristine snorkeling, fewer crowds. Book through your hotel.
- **Koh Lanta's East Coast:** Quieter beaches perfect for swimming and sunset watching.
- **Rai Lay Beach (accessible from Koh Phi Phi):** Rock climbing, kayaking, and excellent seafood restaurants.

Kanchanaburi is Thailand's adventure capital—waterfalls, river rafting, and cave exploration—but it's often overlooked by tourists rushing north. By building it into your week, you get a completely different Thailand experience: fewer tourists, more nature, and authentic village interactions. Our team spent time here and found it to be one of the most rewarding regions for active travelers.

### Day-by-Day Breakdown

**Days 1–2: Bangkok**
Arrive and explore as in the previous itineraries. Day 2 afternoon: take a minivan or train to Kanchanaburi (2–3 hours, ₿100–200).

**Days 3–4: Kanchanaburi**
Spend two full days here. Visit Erawan Waterfall (7-tiered waterfall, entry ₿200, best visited early morning). Take a bamboo raft trip down the Khwae River (₿800–1,200 per person, full day). Visit the Bridge over the River Kwai and War Museum if interested in WWII history. Optional: cave tubing or rock climbing tours (₿1,000–1,500).

**Days 5–7: Chiang Mai**
Overnight bus or train from Kanchanaburi to Chiang Mai (10–12 hours, ₿400–800). Arrive Day 5 morning. Days 5–7: jungle trekking (₿1,000–1,500 for 2-day trek including accommodation), visit hill tribe villages, or take a cooking class. Visit [Chiang Mai attractions](https://go2-thailand.com/city/chiang-mai/attractions/) like Doi Suthep temple and the Old City markets.

## 4. Practical Essentials for Any 7-Day Thailand Trip

### Getting There & Getting Around

Flying into [Bangkok](https://go2-thailand.com/city/bangkok/) is almost always your best option—Suvarnabhumi Airport is Southeast Asia's major hub. From there, you have multiple options:

- **Domestic flights:** Bangkok Airways, Thai AirAsia, and Nok Air connect major cities. Flights to Chiang Mai (2 hours, ₿1,200–2,000) or Phuket (2 hours, ₿1,200–2,000) are quick and affordable.
- **Overnight trains:** The State Railway of Thailand operates sleeper trains to Chiang Mai and southern destinations. Book at railway.co.th or through your hotel.
- **Long-distance buses:** Cheapest option (₿300–800) but slower. Use 12Go Asia for booking.

### Money & Budgeting

Thailand is genuinely affordable. Here's what you'll spend daily:

- **Budget travelers:** ₿1,000–1,500/day ($28–42 USD) — guesthouses, street food, free temples
- **Mid-range:** ₿2,000–3,500/day ($57–100 USD) — 3-star hotels, restaurant meals, paid activities
- **Luxury:** ₿5,000+/day ($140+ USD) — 4-5 star hotels, fine dining, private tours

ATMs are everywhere in cities and towns. Credit cards work in hotels and restaurants but not street stalls. Notify your bank before traveling.

### Internet & Staying Connected

Get a [Thailand eSIM](https://go2-thailand.com/esim/) before arrival or buy a local SIM card at the airport (₿99–299 for 7–30 days of data). AIS, Dtac, and True are the main providers. Most hotels and restaurants offer free WiFi.

### Accommodation Booking

Book hotels 2–3 weeks in advance during peak season (November–February). Prices spike 30–50% during this period. Budget guesthouses (₿300–600/night) are clean and social. Mid-range hotels (₿1,000–2,000/night) offer good value.

### Visa & Entry Requirements

Most Western nationalities get 30–60 days visa-free. Check your specific country's requirements at [Thailand visa information](https://go2-thailand.com/visa/). You must complete the Thailand Digital Arrival Card (TM.6) before arrival—submit it at least 3 days before your flight at tdac.immigration.go.th.

> **Did You Know?** Thailand received over 39 million international visitors in 2019 (pre-pandemic), making it one of the world's top tourism destinations.
>
> *Source: [Tourism Authority of Thailand](https://www.tourismthailand.org/)*

### Best Time to Visit

**November–February:** Cool, dry, perfect weather. Peak season = higher prices.
**March–April:** Hot but manageable. Fewer crowds, lower prices.
**May–October:** Monsoon season. Cheaper, fewer tourists, but expect rain and rough seas.

### What to Pack

- Lightweight, breathable clothing
- Sturdy walking shoes + flip-flops
- Sunscreen (SPF 50+) and hat
- Medications (pharmacies require prescriptions for some drugs)
- Power adapter (Thailand uses 220V, Type A/B plugs)
- Modest clothing for temples (sarongs available for rent)

## 5. Food Highlights for Your Week

No visit to Thailand is complete without eating well. Here's what to try:

**Bangkok:** Street food at Yaowarat (Chinatown) for [pad thai](https://go2-thailand.com/food/pad-thai/) and seafood. [Tom yum goong](https://go2-thailand.com/food/tom-yum-goong/) at local markets. [Thai iced tea](https://go2-thailand.com/drinks/thai-iced-tea/) everywhere.

**Chiang Mai:** [Larb](https://go2-thailand.com/food/larb/) (spicy meat salad), khao soi (curry noodles), and sai oua (local sausage). Night markets offer incredible value—full meals for ₿30–50.

**Islands:** Fresh seafood grilled on the beach. [Green curry](https://go2-thailand.com/food/green-curry/) with local fish. [Mango sticky rice](https://go2-thailand.com/food/mango-sticky-rice/) for dessert.

Take a [cooking class](https://go2-thailand.com/best-cooking-classes-in-thailand/) in Chiang Mai or Bangkok to learn techniques and shop at local markets with your instructor.

## 6. Safety, Scams & Common Sense

Thailand is generally safe for tourists. Violent crime against visitors is rare. However:

- **Gem scams:** Avoid unsolicited gem shop recommendations from tuk-tuk drivers.
- **Drink spiking:** Never leave drinks unattended in bars. Stick to reputable venues.
- **Overcharging:** Always agree on prices before getting in a taxi or tuk-tuk. Use Grab (ride-sharing app) for fixed prices.
- **Valuables:** Don't flash expensive cameras, jewelry, or large amounts of cash.
- **Respect the monarchy:** Never disrespect images of the King. It's illegal and offensive.

Check [Thailand scams & safety guide](https://go2-thailand.com/practical-info/scams-safety/) for detailed information.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I do all three itineraries in one trip?

Not comfortably in 7 days. You'd spend half your time traveling. Pick one route and save the others for future trips. Thailand will still be there!

### What's the cheapest time to visit?

May–October (monsoon season). Prices drop 30–50%, but expect rain and rough seas. September–October offers the best value with improving weather.

### Do I need to book everything in advance?

Flights and hotels during peak season (Nov–Feb): yes, 2–3 weeks ahead. Activities and tours: 1–2 days is usually fine. Trains: book 1–2 weeks ahead for sleepers.

### Is 7 days enough to see Thailand?

Absolutely. You'll see temples, beaches, mountains, and eat incredible food. You won't see *everything*, but you'll get a genuine taste of the country.

### What's the best itinerary for first-timers?

The classic Bangkok → Ayutthaya → Chiang Mai route. It hits all the major cultural highlights and gives you a sense of Thailand's geography and history.

### Can I do this trip solo?

Yes. Thailand is very solo-traveler friendly. Guesthouses have communal areas, group tours are easy to join, and locals are welcoming. Women solo travelers should take standard precautions (see Safety section).
