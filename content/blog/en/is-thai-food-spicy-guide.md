## Is Thai Food Spicy?

One of the most common questions from first-time visitors to **Thailand** is: "Is the food going to be too spicy for me?" The short answer is that **Thai food ranges from completely mild to scorchingly hot**, and you have far more control over the spice level than you might think. This guide breaks down the myth, explains regional differences, and gives you the phrases you need to order comfortably. For a broader look at what to eat, visit our [Thai food section](/food/).

### Key Takeaways

| Question | Quick Answer |
|----------|-------------|
| Is all Thai food spicy? | **No.** Many popular dishes like Pad Thai and Khao Man Gai have zero heat. |
| Which region is the spiciest? | **Southern Thailand** and **Isaan (Northeast)** serve the hottest dishes. |
| Can I ask for less spice? | Yes. Say **"mai pet"** (ไม่เผ็ด) to request no spice. |

## Not All Thai Food Is Spicy: Myth vs Reality

There is a widespread belief that every Thai dish will set your mouth on fire. This is a **myth**. While chili peppers are a cornerstone of Thai cooking, the cuisine is built around a careful balance of **sweet, sour, salty, bitter, and spicy** flavours. Many dishes lean heavily on the first four and use little to no chili at all.

Thai cooks understand that not everyone shares the same tolerance for heat. In tourist areas especially, restaurants routinely adjust dishes to suit foreign palates. Even at local street stalls, you can request a milder version of most meals. For tips on navigating street stalls, see our guide to [Bangkok street food](/blog/bangkok-street-food-beginners/).

## Spice Levels by Region

Thai cuisine is not one monolithic style. **Each region has its own heat profile**, shaped by local ingredients and cultural influences.

### Southern Thailand -- The Hottest

Southern Thai food is considered the **spiciest in the country**. Dishes like **Gaeng Tai Pla** (fish organ curry) and **Khua Kling** (dry curry) use generous amounts of chili paste. The heat is intense and immediate. The region's proximity to Malaysia and India has also introduced additional spice-heavy cooking techniques.

### Northeastern Thailand (Isaan) -- Fiery and Bold

**Isaan cuisine** is famous for dishes like **Som Tam** (green papaya salad) and **Larb** (minced meat salad), both of which can be extremely hot. Street vendors in Isaan often add handfuls of Bird's Eye chilies by default. If you visit this region, always specify your preferred spice level.

### Central Thailand -- Balanced Heat

Central Thai food, including what most visitors encounter in **Bangkok**, strikes a balance between all five flavour profiles. Dishes like **Green Curry** and **Tom Yum** are spicy but not overwhelmingly so. This is where you will find the most flexibility in adjusting heat levels.

### Northern Thailand -- The Mildest

Northern Thai cooking is the **mildest of the four regions**, with roots in Burmese culinary traditions. Dishes like **Khao Soi** (coconut curry noodles) and **Sai Oua** (herb sausage) use aromatic spices and herbs more than raw chili heat. If you have a low spice tolerance, Northern Thailand is your best friend. Learn more in our [Chiang Mai guide](/city/chiang-mai/).

## Non-Spicy Thai Dishes You Should Try

If you want to avoid spice entirely, these dishes are reliably mild:

- **Pad Thai** -- Stir-fried rice noodles with tamarind sauce, egg, and your choice of protein. Thailand's most famous dish is naturally mild.
- **Khao Man Gai** -- Poached chicken served over fragrant rice with a ginger-garlic dipping sauce. A comforting, zero-heat dish.
- **Tom Kha Gai** -- Coconut milk soup with chicken, galangal, and lemongrass. Creamy and aromatic rather than spicy.
- **Pad See Ew** -- Wide rice noodles stir-fried with soy sauce, Chinese broccoli, and egg. Sweet and savoury with no chili.
- **Mango Sticky Rice** -- Sweet sticky rice with fresh mango and coconut cream. The perfect mild dessert.

## How to Order Less Spicy in Thailand

Knowing a few Thai phrases will save you a lot of discomfort:

| Thai Phrase | Pronunciation | Meaning |
|-------------|---------------|---------|
| ไม่เผ็ด | **Mai pet** | Not spicy |
| เผ็ดนิดหน่อย | **Pet nit noi** | A little spicy |
| เผ็ดปานกลาง | **Pet paan glaang** | Medium spicy |
| เผ็ดมาก | **Pet maak** | Very spicy |

Most restaurants and street vendors will understand **"mai pet"** immediately. Some may still add a small amount of chili for flavour, but it will be significantly toned down. If you want absolutely zero chili, you can add **"mai sai prik"** (ไม่ใส่พริก), which means "don't add chili."

## Thai Chili Types: Know What You Are Eating

Thailand uses several types of chili peppers, each with different heat levels:

- **Prik Kee Noo (Bird's Eye Chili)** -- The most common and hottest everyday chili. Ranges from **50,000 to 100,000 SHU** on the Scoville scale, roughly 15 times hotter than a jalapeno.
- **Prik Chee Fah (Sky-Pointing Chili)** -- A longer, milder chili ranging from **5,000 to 30,000 SHU**. Often used in salads, soups, and stir-fries. Has a slightly sweet flavour.
- **Prik Jinda** -- A medium-heat chili around **75,000 SHU**, commonly used in curry pastes.
- **Prik Noom** -- A large, mild green chili popular in Northern Thai cuisine. Roasted and served as a dip.
- **Dried chilies (Prik Haeng)** -- Used in stir-fries and curry pastes. The heat varies depending on the variety.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is Pad Thai spicy?

**No.** Traditional Pad Thai is a sweet-and-sour dish with no chili in the base recipe. You will usually find chili flakes on the table as an optional condiment.

### Will Thai restaurants make food less spicy if I ask?

**Yes.** Thai cooks are very accustomed to adjusting spice levels, especially in tourist areas. Simply say "mai pet" when ordering.

### Is Thai food spicier in Thailand than in Western restaurants?

**Generally, yes.** Thai restaurants outside of Thailand often tone down the heat for local tastes. In Thailand, the default spice level at local eateries can be significantly higher than what you are used to.

### What should I do if a dish is too spicy?

Drink **plain rice** or eat **steamed rice** alongside the dish. Rice is far more effective at neutralising capsaicin than water. A spoonful of **sugar** or a sip of **coconut milk** can also help.

### Can children eat Thai food?

**Absolutely.** Dishes like Khao Man Gai, Pad See Ew, and fried rice are all child-friendly and contain no spice. Most Thai families feed their young children these same dishes.
